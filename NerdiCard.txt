1. Introduction

NERDICARD is a mathematically driven strategic card game in which players construct,
manipulate, and attack mathematical expressions through the use of function components
grounded in calculus, number theory, and linear algebra. Players build functions across
multiple boards, utilize advanced mathematical transformations and theorems to compro-
mise opponents' functions or health points (HP), and evaluate expressions strategically
to gain advantages. This rulebook details the game's components, setup, mechanics, and
victory conditions.

2. Game Components

2.1. Card Types

The game utilizes three principal decks:

• Function              Component Cards (FCC): These cards represent mathematical func-
       tions and operations used to construct and modify player functions. They include
      base functions, calculus operations, number theory operations, and linear algebra
expansions.
• Number             Deck: Contains constants, prime numbers limited to the first three
primes, well-known irrational numbers, and arithmetic operation cards.
• Action        Deck: Contains offensive, defensive, trap, spell, and theorem cards, as
        well as special action cards such as board additions, function composition, and
evaluation.

2.2. Players' Hands and Boards

Each player manages:

• A hand  of cards drawn from the decks.
• Up to    three Function Boards, each corresponding to a specific function or function
space (e.g., scalar-valued, vector-valued, or matrix-valued).
• A set          of Variable Cards and Number Cards allocated at the start of the game.

3. Game Setup

3.1. Player Initialization

Each player receives the following before the game begins:

• Exactly         one Base Function Component Card, representing their initial function
domain. Possible base domains include:
- Rational functions (including polynomials of degree up to five)
        - Trigonometric functions (up to six trig terms combined via addition or multi-
plication)
             - Exponential and logarithmic functions (up to ten combined logs and expo-
nents)


3

• Ten       unique Variable Cards, labeled x1, x2, . . . , x10. Each variable card may be
used once per function construction phase.
• Ten         Number Cards, consisting of the primes 2, 3, and 5, and seven distinguished
irrational numbers (e.g., π, e, √   2, √
    3, φ, γ, log 2).
• Five        randomly drawn starter cards from a combination of FCC, Action, and Defense
decks.

3.2. Function Construction

Players construct their initial function according to the restrictions and allowances of
their base card domain, using their variables and numbers.

3.3. Boards

Players begin with one active function board corresponding to their base card's domain.
Additional boards may be added during gameplay via Add Board cards, including new
boards for linear algebra mechanics.

3.4. Decks and Draw Piles

Players maintain three decks during gameplay:
• FCC  Deck: Contains function component cards.
• Number   Deck: Contains number and arithmetic operation cards.
• Action            Deck: Contains attacks, defenses, theorems, traps, and special action
cards.

Players draw from these decks during their turns, as specified in the turn structure.

4. Turn Structure and Gameplay

4.1. Turn Order

Players alternate turns in a clockwise order. Each turn consists of the following phases:

             1. Draw Phase: The active player draws two cards from any combination of the
three decks.

2. Play Phase: The player may perform up to two actions. Valid actions include:
• Building     or modifying their function(s) using FCC cards.
• Playing     attack cards targeting opponent functions or HP.
• Playing     defense, trap, or theorem cards.
• Playing          special action cards (e.g., Add Board, Composition, Force Evaluation).
• Evaluating   their function.

           3. Defense Phase: Other players may respond immediately to attacks or plays by
       using defense or trap cards. Each player may play only one such card per triggered
event.

          4. Resolution Phase: All played cards are resolved and any used cards are moved
to the graveyard (the 3rd Dimension).

    4

4.2. Action Restrictions
• Players may only perform one offensive action per turn.
• Trap cards occupy a dedicated trap slot; setting a trap prohibits playing an offensive
card during that turn.
• Defense    and trap cards may be played reactively on any player's turn.
• Players may maintain up to three function boards concurrently.

5. Function Construction and Modification

5.1. General Restrictions
• Variables       can only be used once per function construction phase and are released
upon function evaluation.
• Number     cards can be reused unless specified otherwise.
• Functions  must conform to the domain specified by the player's base card.
• Constants        used in polynomial terms are restricted to integers between -5 and 5,
excluding zero unless explicitly allowed.

5.2. Base Domains and Limitations

Rational Functions: Polynomials of degree up to five. Rational functions consist of a
          numerator and denominator, each a polynomial of degree at most five. Constants
       in terms must be within the allowed integer range and used at most once per term.
Trigonometric Functions: Functions may include up to six trigonometric terms (sine,
     cosine, tangent, etc.) combined by addition or multiplication only. No compositions
are permitted.
Exponential and Logarithmic Functions: Functions may contain up to ten com-
      bined logarithmic and exponential terms with bases 2, 10, or e. A logarithm raised
              to a power counts as two terms toward the limit. Terms may be combined via
addition, multiplication, or division.

5.3. Multiple Boards and Composition

Players may possess up to three function boards, each with its own function. Players may
use Composition Cards to substitute one board's function into a variable of another
board's function, with the following rules:

• Compositions       may only occur between boards with different base function domains.
• Nested  composition depth is limited to two.

6. Card Types and Effects

6.1. Function Component Cards (FCCs)
FCCs include:

• Add               Term Cards: Add terms to the function in compliance with base domain
rules.


5

• Calculus   FCCs: Enable differentiation, integration (indefinite and definite), limit
evaluation, and continuity manipulations.
• Number              Theory FCCs: Include operations such as modular arithmetic, prime
factor manipulation, and theorems influencing function coefficients and variables.
• Linear        Algebra FCCs: Facilitate vector and matrix operations, linear transfor-
mations, and rank and eigenvalue manipulations.

6.2. Action Cards
Action cards include:

• Offensive  attack cards targeting opponent functions or HP.
• Defense and shield cards protecting functions or HP.
• Trap  cards that activate in response to opponent actions.
• Theorem     cards granting strategic effects, categorized as Martial Theorems (offen-
sive) and Artifact Theorems (defensive or passive).
• Special action cards such as Add Board, Composition, and Force Evaluation.

7. Mathematical Mechanics

7.1. Calculus
• Differentiation  cards may target entire functions or specific terms, applying power,
chain, product, or quotient rules.
• Integration      cards come in two types: indefinite and definite. Definite integrals
require bounds supplied from the player's hand but do not consume those cards.
• Recursive    integration (multiple integrations with respect to one or multiple vari-
ables) is permitted.
• If a  player suspects a function is not integrable, they may challenge with an "Inte-
grable?" card once every three rounds. Failure to integrate causes HP penalty to
the challenger.
• Limit   evaluation cards allow evaluation of function limits at specified points. Un-
defined or infinite limits cause destruction of the function board or immediate loss
if integral to the player's survival.

7.2. Number Theory
• Players may manipulate coefficients and variables using modular arithmetic.
• Prime-based cards can alter constants or act as multipliers.
• Theorems         such as Euler's theorem, Fermat's little theorem, and others provide
offensive and defensive effects.

7.3. Linear Algebra
• Players                  may add Vector Space Boards or Matrix Boards using Add Board
cards.
• Functions  on these boards may be vector-valued or matrix-valued.
• Players may apply linear transformations such as rotations, reflections, projections,
and scalings.

   • Rank         and nullity can be manipulated to affect the function's complexity and vul-
   nerability.
   • Eigenvalue and eigenvector cards confer scaling effects or protection.
   • Determinant and inverse operations are used to attack or defend matrix boards.
   • Players   may solve systems of linear equations via card effects to isolate variables or
   reduce opponent HP.

   8. Health Points and Function Evaluation

   8.1. Initial HP and Evaluation
   Each player starts the game with zero health points (HP). Players increase their HP by
   evaluating their functions.
   Evaluation requires:
   • A Variable  Value Card to substitute variables.
   • The  player calculates the function's value at the substituted point.
   • HP  gained is proportional to the function value and its complexity.

   8.2. HP Gain Formula

   The health points gained from evaluating a function are calculated as:

       HP Gain = Function Value ×     Complexity Score,
       10
   where
   • The  Complexity Score is the sum of:
   - +1 for each distinct variable term,
   - +1 for each additional term beyond the first,
   - +2 for each composition applied.
   • Functions        with fewer than two variable terms are ineligible to gain HP upon eval-
uation.

   8.3. Function Evaluation and Aftereffects
   • Evaluated       functions are removed from the board and their cards reshuffled into the
   FCC deck.
   • Variables used are returned to the player's hand.

   8.4. Force Evaluation
   A Force Evaluation card compels all players to evaluate their functions simultaneously.
   If the initiator's evaluated function value is greater than twice the value of every other
   player, the initiator wins immediately.
   Otherwise:
   • Half of the initiator's HP is evenly distributed among all players.
   • The       remaining half is distributed among players whose evaluated values exceed that
   of the initiator.
   • The  initiator's function is destroyed, and no HP is gained.

8.5. Stalling Prevention

If no player evaluates their function within 20 turns globally or if five consecutive turns
pass without any evaluation, all players must evaluate at the next opportunity.

9. Victory Conditions

A player wins the game by achieving any one of the following conditions:
1. Reducing all opposing players' HP to zero.
         2. Forcing an opponent's function to isolate a single variable and preventing its re-
building within three turns.
            3. Winning by force evaluation domination (initiator's evaluated function value is
greater than twice every other's).
              4. Reducing an opponent's vector space dimension to zero or forcing their matrix
board to be singular (non-invertible).

10. Additional Rules and Clarifications

10.1. Undefined and Infinite Evaluations

If evaluation yields an undefined or infinite value, the affected function board is destroyed.
Undefined evaluation results in immediate loss of the game.

10.2. Function Isolation and Rebuilding
A player with an isolated variable function must reconstruct a valid function conforming
to base domain rules within three turns or lose the game.

10.3. Card Rarity

Cards are classified by rarity and strength:
• Common Cards: Basic FCCs, numbers, and simple actions.
• Rare               Cards: Composite functions, specialized calculus operations, and mid-tier
theorems.
• Ultra               Rare Cards: Board addition, composition, force evaluation, advanced the-
orems, and linear algebra mechanics.

11. Glossary

Board: A designated play area representing a single function or function space.
Function Component Card (FCC): A card used to build or modify functions.
Variable Card: Card representing a variable symbol for function construction.
Number Card: Card representing a numeric constant or arithmetic operator.
Evaluation: The process of substituting variables with values to compute function value
and gain HP.
Trap: A card that responds to opponent actions by negating or altering effects.
Force Evaluation: A special card compelling all players to evaluate functions simulta-
neously.
